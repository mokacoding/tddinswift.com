When *Test-Driven Development in Swift* first shipped in 2021,
XCTest was the only game in town and Combine was how you bound a `ViewModel` to a SwiftUI view.
Five years on, neither of those is true.

[Apple announced Swift Testing in 2023](https://forums.swift.org/t/a-new-approach-to-testing-in-swift/67425),
and [as of Xcode 16, Swift Testing is the default for new projects](https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2024/10179/?time=103).
Structured concurrency replaced Combine for most of the work a `ViewModel` does asynchronously.
SwiftUI itself moved on, and iOS 26 brought a refreshed design language.

That gap is what the second edition is for.

## The three big changes

### 1. Swift Testing throughout

Every test in the book is now written with Swift Testing.
`@Suite struct` instead of `XCTestCase` subclasses.
`@Test func` instead of `func testÔÇª()`.
`#expect` and `#require` instead of `XCTAssert*` and `XCTUnwrap`.
`Issue.record` instead of `XCTFail`.
`confirmation` instead of `XCTestExpectation`.

The Test-Driven Development *process* is identical &mdash;
red, green, refactor;
small steps;
the compiler as a feedback loop.
The vocabulary changes;
the discipline does not.

Chapter 2 is now a Swift Testing introduction.
For anyone working in a codebase that still uses XCTest,
the new Appendix B keeps a complete XCTest reference and shows worked examples in both frameworks side by side.
You can keep shipping XCTest at work while reading the book in Swift Testing.

### 2. Structured concurrency where Combine used to live

The networking layer and the asynchronous chapters have been rewritten with `async`/`await`.
The original `AnyPublisher<T, Error>` signatures are now `async throws -> T`.
The test-side patterns &mdash; how you await a sequence of state changes,
how you confirm a callback fired exactly once &mdash;
all use Swift Testing's `confirmation` and `await`,
not `XCTestExpectation` and `sink`.

If you've ever had a Combine test pass for the wrong reason because a `sink` swallowed an error,
the async/await rewrite will feel like coming up for air.

### 3. SwiftUI and iOS 26

The sample app's UI has been refreshed to match the latest SwiftUI APIs and the iOS 26 visual language.
The data-flow patterns now show `@Observable` alongside `@Published`,
and there's a sidebar in Chapter 7 that walks through the differences.

## What's new content-wise

**An interlude on LLMs and Test-Driven Development.**
Between Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 sits a new short chapter on coding with AI assistants.
The argument: whether a line of code is written one keystroke at a time or generated by an agent,
tests remain an excellent ally to drive good software design.
They also act as encoded specifications of the software's expected behavior,
doubling as a safety net against an agent implementing the wrong change.

**An expanded Chapter 16 conclusion.**
The first edition's closing chapter was a paragraph and a wave goodbye.
The new conclusion has dedicated sections on TDD as a design tool,
TDD and productivity,
and TDD and product development.
It's the section that ties the book's specific techniques back to the broader practice.

**A "Where to go from here" appendix.**
A pragmatic survey of what comes after unit tests:
continuous integration,
snapshot testing,
UI testing,
API integration testing,
modularization for faster feedback,
and an honest discussion of how to convince your team to embrace the practice.
Each topic gets enough material to know what you're looking at;
the appendix is a launchpad,
not an encyclopedia.

**A short note on indentation and method names.**
Small, but the kind of thing that quietly shapes how readable a test suite is.

## What stayed the same

The menu-ordering sample app is still the spine of the book.
The progression through the test-double zoo &mdash; Stub, Spy, Fake, Dummy &mdash; is unchanged in shape.
Chapter 5 still introduces fixtures the same way.
The fizz-buzz worked example in Chapter 3 still teaches the rhythm of red/green/refactor.

The Test-Driven Development philosophy hasn't changed,
because TDD wasn't tied to a framework in the first place.

## Should you buy v2 if you already own v1?

A few honest answers.

**Buy it if** you're moving (or about to move) a codebase from XCTest to Swift Testing
and want a guided tour of the new patterns in context,
not just an API reference.

**Buy it if** you write Swift but haven't yet internalized structured concurrency,
and you'd like the networking and async-update chapters rewritten with `async`/`await` from the ground up.

**Buy it if** the LLM interlude or the expanded "Where to go from here" appendix sounds like the chapter you've been wishing existed.

**You're probably fine on v1 if** you already write Swift Testing fluently,
your codebase has been on `async`/`await` since the day it shipped,
and you mainly used the book as a reference for the design philosophy.
The philosophy didn't change.
Buying v2 in that case is a vote of confidence more than a learning purchase &mdash;
and I appreciate it,
but I'd rather you knew.
